Competitor analysis
Core deliverable
The most critical study in market research. Knowing who owns the market, their scale, and their clients tells you where the white space is — and what it costs to win.
Why this matters Entering a market without a deep competitor map is the single most common and costly mistake in international expansion. Revenue scale tells you how funded and entrenched they are. Geography reveals gaps. Key clients show you which customer segments are locked up. Without this, you're pricing, positioning, and pitching blind.

Research a specific competitor
1
Identify all competitors
List every direct and adjacent player operating in your target geography. Include startups, incumbents, and adjacent platforms. Use Google, LinkedIn, Crunchbase, and industry association directories as starting points.
2
Revenue & funding scale
Find last reported revenue, latest funding round, or valuation. Primary sources: Crunchbase, Pitchbook, Companies House (UK), Orbis (global), SEC EDGAR (US public), annual reports. For private companies, use LinkedIn headcount as a proxy.
3
Operating geography
Map exactly where each competitor operates — countries, states, cities. Note gaps between their coverage and your target markets. Job postings are one of the most reliable real-time signals for geographic expansion.
4
Key clients
Identify their biggest named clients via press releases, case studies, LinkedIn endorsements, G2 reviews, or SEC filings. This reveals which customer segments are already locked up and where you can differentiate.
5
Official websites & digital presence
Record official website, app store presence, and social following. App reviews on iOS/Android are gold — they surface unmet needs your product can address.
6
Additional intelligence (on demand)
For deeper dives — pricing models, tech stack, NPS estimates, employee churn — use the research prompt box to generate sourced findings on any specific competitor dimension.

Market size & structure
Quantitative
TAM, SAM, and SOM with verified references. Built bottom-up from fleet data, or top-down from industry reports.
Why this matters Market size anchors every business decision that follows — pricing, team sizing, investor narrative, and go/no-go. A market that looks big on a headline TAM slide may have a SAM so fragmented that unit economics never work. Build from verified data, not analyst estimates.
TAM
Total addressable market
Use industry reports (IBIS World, Statista, Grand View Research, Allied MR) or build bottom-up: fleet size × average revenue per vehicle relocation × annual turnover rate. Source: ACEA, national road authorities, JARA, ARN.
SAM
Serviceable addressable market
Filter TAM to geographies and customer segments you can realistically reach within 18 months. Apply your scoring model (volume, purchasing power, velocity, logistics density) to weight markets.
SOM
Serviceable obtainable market
Your realistic capture rate in year 1, given competitive density, your go-to-market capacity, and comparable ramp rates from markets you already operate in.
Ask a market size question
📚
Verified public sources
Every data point in your report must have a stable, citable URL. Decisions built on unverified data fail at due diligence.
Why this matters Investors, boards, and partners will ask "where does this number come from?" If your answer is "a market research firm estimated it," that's a weak foundation. Building from primary sources (government databases, association statistics, official filings) makes your research defensible and credible.

Customer validation
Before capital deployment
Confirm that real demand exists, willingness to pay aligns with your model, and the buying process is what you assumed it to be.
Why this matters Markets that look attractive on paper often have entrenched buying habits, procurement cycles of 6–18 months, or a willingness-to-pay that doesn't cover your unit cost. 10 structured customer interviews cost almost nothing. A failed market entry costs everything.
1
Target customer interviews — minimum 10
Structured conversations with decision-makers in your target segment. Record current solutions, pain points, switching costs, and budget ranges. Do not pitch — just listen.
2
Willingness to pay
Use Van Westendorp pricing questions or Jobs-to-be-Done framing to anchor your pricing before building a P&L. "What would you expect to pay?" is not enough — you need the floor, ceiling, and sweet spot.
3
Letter of intent or paid pilot
A soft verbal yes is not validation. A signed LOI, a paid pilot, or a committed first customer is. Target at least 1 LOI before proceeding to Phase 3.
4
Buying process mapping
Who approves the purchase? How long does procurement take? What alternatives are they currently comparing you against? This shapes your sales motion for the entire market.

Legal & operational validation
High risk area
Your business model may be nationally legal but blocked at state/local level — especially for freelance or gig operating models.
Why this matters Legal misclassification of workers (employee vs. independent contractor) is the most common operational risk for platform businesses expanding internationally. In some U.S. states (California, Massachusetts, New Jersey), the ABC test creates significant exposure. Discovering this after your first hire is expensive. Discovering it after building a team is catastrophic.
!
Freelance classification risk — Review ABC test exposure, Prop 22 analogues, and local labour rulings before a single hire. Engage local counsel. Cost: ~$2–5k. Cost of getting it wrong: 7 figures.
~
Licensing & permits — Identify every licence required (vehicle transport broker, insurance, data handling/GDPR equivalent). Map requirements by state and country.
~
Insurance & liability — Confirm that fleet insurance, driver liability cover, and damage waiver products are available in the new market at viable cost per trip.
✓
Test plate & transit plate regulations — In automotive logistics, confirm whether dealer plates or transit plates are available, and what cross-state or cross-border rules apply to vehicles in transit.
✓
Data residency & privacy — Confirm data localisation requirements (GDPR in EU, CCPA in California, LGPD in Brazil). Platform businesses handling driver and customer data face real penalties for non-compliance.

Unit economics validation
Go / no-go gate
Expansion decisions must be backed by evidence that the core transaction is profitable at realistic volume — not just in a spreadsheet best case.
Why this matters Many companies expand with a business model that worked at home, only to discover that local labour costs, fuel prices, or driver acquisition costs make the unit economics negative. Validating this before scaling prevents expensive reversals.
1
Revenue per trip / per vehicle
Benchmark against comparable markets you already operate in. Account for distance distribution, fleet type mix, and urgency premium in the new market.
2
Driver / freelancer acquisition cost
Model cost per verified driver onboarded in the new market — including background checks, insurance onboarding, and first-trip support. Compare to your home market baseline.
3
Return logistics cost
Calculate return cost per km using local transport benchmarks. Long-distance states (California avg 442km between cities) have asymmetric return cost profiles that compress margins if not modelled explicitly.
4
Payback period
How many trips does a newly onboarded driver need to complete before you recover acquisition cost? This drives your launch cluster density strategy.

Market prioritisation
Strategic
Sequence markets by effort-to-return ratio, not just market size. Entering too many markets simultaneously is one of the most common causes of expansion failure.
Why this matters Every market you enter consumes legal setup time, management attention, and capital. A single well-executed market entry generates references and revenue that fund the next. Five underfunded simultaneous entries generate five half-built presences and five sets of learnings too thin to act on.

Go-to-market localisation
Pre-launch
A copy-paste GTM from your home market ignores local buyer behaviour, pricing norms, and competitive dynamics — the three factors most likely to kill a launch.
Why this matters What worked in Finland or Poland will not automatically work in the US, Brazil, or Japan. Pricing must reflect local median wages and petrol costs. Sales channels must match how buyers actually buy. Driver recruitment must use channels that exist in that market. Localisation is not translation — it's rebuilding the GTM from local data.
1
Local partner mapping
Identify dealer groups, auction houses, leasing associations, and fleet managers as channel partners. The fastest path to volume in a new market is almost always through a large existing player — not direct sales from scratch.
2
Pricing localisation
Adjust pricing to local median wages, petrol costs, return logistics costs, and competitor rate cards. Do not import EUR pricing directly to USD markets — parity pricing is rarely correct.
3
Driver supply strategy
Map local freelancer platforms, automotive job boards, and dealership networks as driver recruitment channels. In each market, one channel typically dominates — find it before launch.
4
Regulatory playbook
Document the specific licence, insurance, and plate setup required per market. Assign owners and deadlines. Nothing delays a market launch more predictably than a regulatory step that was assumed rather than confirmed.
